TILT: Transparency in Learning & Teaching

The TILTed@Southampton methodology for clarifying and communicating assessment purpose. Evidence-based, proven to improve student success, and designed to reduce inequity — particularly for underrepresented student groups.

What is TILT?

TILT (Transparency in Learning and Teaching) is an evidence-based approach developed by Mary Ann Winkelmes, shown to improve student success and reduce inequities in higher education. Students routinely report finding assessment expectations difficult to grasp, even where guidance has been provided — TILT addresses this by making explicit the implicit.

The University of Southampton uses a localised version called TILTed@Southampton. By making small but meaningful adjustments to how assessments are conveyed to students, this methodology helps to:

  • Improve student understanding of expectations
  • Strengthen links between academic learning and future goals
  • Foster belonging and confidence
  • Promote equity of opportunity for all learners

TILT has particular benefits for underrepresented student groups. Evidence shows that TILTing even just one assessment helps students recognise that assessments do in fact have a purpose — an insight they carry across into other assessments.

The Three Components

Every TILTed assessment brief must make three things explicitly and separately clear. These are not sections of a document to tick off — they are a discipline of thinking that forces staff to surface what is often implicit.

P — Purpose

Why does this assessment exist? What knowledge and skills will students develop by completing it?

T — Task

A complete, not partial, set of instructions. Students should not have to guess or infer what they are being asked to do.

C — Criteria for Success

Clear, specific pointers on what markers are looking for, tied to real-world or professional contexts.

Purpose — in detail

The PURPOSE section should answer: why does this assessment exist? What knowledge and skills will students have the opportunity to develop by completing it?

  • Written in second person (e.g. "By completing this assessment you will develop...")
  • Kept concise — limited to the most important knowledge and skills
  • Must surface the implicit rationale that staff hold but rarely share with students
  • Should include how this assessment connects to the student's wider learning journey, related modules, and future career
  • Headers "Knowledge gained" and "Skills gained" can help structure this section

Critical point: PURPOSE is not a restatement of the TASK. It is the genuine pedagogical reason behind setting the assessment — the "why" rather than the "what." This is the component staff most often get wrong, either omitting it entirely or conflating it with the task description.

✗ Wrong: "Purpose: Students will write a 2,500-word essay on X." (This is the task, not the purpose.)
✓ Right: "Purpose: By completing this assessment you will develop your ability to synthesise primary and secondary sources, construct an evidence-based argument, and communicate complex ideas in writing — skills central to professional practice in this field."

Task — in detail

The TASK section must be a complete, not partial, set of instructions. Students should not have to guess or infer what they are being asked to do.

  • All instructions included — not spread across multiple documents or communicated verbally only
  • If an open-ended or creative component requires students to figure something out independently, this must be stated explicitly so students know the ambiguity is intentional
  • Supplementary detail (appendices, marking grids, further guidance) should be in a linked appendix, not the main brief

Students handle intentional ambiguity far better when they know it is deliberate — they can focus on working it out rather than worrying that they are missing something.

Criteria for Success — in detail

The CRITERIA FOR SUCCESS section should give students clear, specific pointers on what markers are looking for.

  • Tied to the bigger picture — not just "use references correctly" but why this skill matters
  • Linked to real-world or professional contexts — what industry or professional practice looks like in this area
  • Connected to the Successful Future Skills model where possible
  • Avoid vague descriptors — be specific about what distinguishes good work from excellent work
  • Supplementary guidance goes in a linked appendix, not the main brief

Real world examples help students grasp the skills they need to develop by relating them to the real world. These should focus on the primary skills or those being developed across multiple modules.

The Student Co-Design Step

After drafting the TILTed brief, teams must discuss it with students in an early session to check what students actually understand the brief to be asking. This is not optional — it is what makes TILT effective rather than merely well-intentioned.

Session lengths of 15, 30, or 50 minutes work depending on context. The brief should then be updated to address any misunderstandings before the assessment goes live.

This step is important because staff often feel they have provided clear assessment instructions — but students routinely interpret briefs differently from the intended meaning. Hearing from students allows teams to clarify any misunderstandings early on, reducing queries later.

"What do your students make of your brief? What do they think it is asking them to do? Hearing from them allows you to clarify any misunderstandings early on."

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Too much information in the main brief. Giving too much information is a common mistake when first trying TILT. Supplementary detail belongs in a linked appendix.
  • PURPOSE written as a restatement of TASK. The purpose is the genuine pedagogical reason — not a description of what students will produce.
  • Skipping the student conversation step. Without hearing from students, you cannot know whether the brief achieves what you intended.
  • Criteria that are vague or unmeasurable. "Good critical analysis" is not a criterion for success — describe what that looks like in practice.

Evidence & Case Study

TILT is a research-driven methodology proven to be effective in a higher education context. It helps all students but has particular benefits for underrepresented student groups.

Case Study — Southampton

Enhancing Assessment Clarity in a Large Nursing / Allied Health Module

Written by Dr Chris Allen, Principal Teaching Fellow, Adult Nursing Lead, School of Health Sciences

Context

A third-year module with over 400 students across multiple nursing and allied health programmes needed clearer alignment between the assessment brief, programme outcomes, and industry expectations. Consultancy support began just one week before teaching, and collaborative work between the Programme Lead and the Assessment Consultancy enabled rapid implementation.

What they did

The assessment brief was restructured so that expectations were clearly articulated for students and markers, ensuring greater clarity and alignment across the assessment, outcomes, and professional relevance.

Impact

  • Students expressed greater confidence in understanding what was required
  • Improved quality and consistency of submissions
  • More efficient and consistent marking across the teaching team
  • Far fewer queries from students seeking clarification
  • Reduced administrative burden — TILT streamlined preparation rather than adding workload

The internal moderator commended: the clarity of briefing provided to students, markers, and the moderator; assessment guidance that was "notably enhanced"; and a more typical and representative distribution of marks, with fewer extreme marks compared with previous years.

What they learned

"Adopting TILT did not take excessive time to implement, nor did it detract from teaching; instead, it streamlined preparation and reduced the administrative burden."

ADA Prompt for TILT / G3

When assessment purpose communication becomes relevant in a conversation, ADA asks:

"Does each assessment brief explicitly and separately state its purpose, a complete task description, and clear criteria for success? Have students been given the opportunity to respond to the brief and have misunderstandings been addressed before the assessment goes live? Are real-world connections made explicit in the criteria for success?"
