Collaborative Assessment Design Decision Making Framework

The four-phase iterative framework guiding ADA's facilitation. Phases inform and may trigger revision of previous phases — organic flow between phases is encouraged.

Phases Phase 1: Foundation & Purposes Phase 2: Design & Alignment Phase 3: Validity, Fairness & Integrity Phase 4: Design & Build Principles Implementation Principles Using the Framework

This framework operates as an iterative cycle, not a linear process. Each phase informs and can trigger revision of previous phases. ADA allows organic conversation flow between phases rather than rigid systematic progression. ADA does not explicitly name which phase the conversation is in unless participants ask.

Phase 1

Foundation and Purposes

Core Components

Define Purposes: Formative assessment, summative assessment, ipsative assessment, diagnostic assessment, and other assessment types as relevant.

Analyse Constraints: Programme context, institutional policies, available resources, time limitations, technical requirements.

Mutual Understanding — ILOs in Varying Contexts: Learner characteristics, student assessment experiences across course and programme level, career aspirations, cultural relevance, application of learning.

Key Reflection Questions

  • How would students like to apply their learning?
  • How have students developed the disciplinary core ideas, cross-cutting concepts and skills?
  • How do students make use of feedback?
  • What interactions and relationships foster learning?
Phase 1 Checklist
  • Assessment purposes clearly defined
  • Constraints identified and documented
  • Student contexts and needs understood
  • Learning outcomes articulated in student-friendly language
Phase 2

Design and Alignment

Identify Targeted Performances and Expectations

  • Develop marking criteria
  • Define performance standards
  • Establish clear expectations

Unpack Disciplinary Elements

  • Core Ideas: Fundamental concepts within the discipline
  • Practices: Discipline-specific methods and approaches
  • Cross-cutting Concepts and Skills: Transferable competencies

Design Tasks and Activities

  • Align with learning outcomes
  • Consider authentic assessment opportunities
  • Build in complexity progression

Plan Feedback Process

  • Timing of feedback
  • Types of feedback
  • Feedback mechanisms
  • Feed-forward opportunities

Define Interactions

  • Teacher-Student: Direct instruction, guidance, support
  • Student-Student: Peer learning, collaboration, peer assessment

Key Reflection Questions

  • How do students currently engage with disciplinary core ideas and practices?
  • What types of feedback will be most valuable for learning?
  • What interaction patterns best support the learning objectives?
  • How can peer learning enhance the assessment experience?
Phase 2 Checklist
  • Performance criteria developed
  • Disciplinary elements unpacked
  • Tasks designed and aligned
  • Feedback process planned
  • Interaction patterns defined
Phase 3

Validating Assessment for Validity, Fairness and Integrity

Validity Check

  • Do tasks enable students to demonstrate their ILOs fairly and inclusively?
  • Are assessment methods appropriate for the learning outcomes?
  • Is there alignment between teaching, learning, and assessment?

Curriculum Support Analysis

  • Is the course curriculum adequately preparing students for the assessment tasks?
  • Are there gaps between what is taught and what is assessed?
  • Do students have opportunities to practise before summative assessment?

Feedback and Grading Evaluation

  • Do the feedback mechanisms serve their intended purpose?
  • Are grading criteria transparent and consistently applied?
  • Does the assessment provide meaningful information about student learning?

Key Reflection Questions

  • Are there barriers for students to engage with the assessment tasks?
  • What pedagogical activities are deliberately designed to remove those barriers?
  • How do teachers make use of feedback and grades to inform their teaching?
  • Is the assessment inclusive of diverse learning styles and backgrounds?
Phase 3 Checklist
  • Validity confirmed
  • Fairness evaluated
  • Integrity measures in place
  • Curriculum alignment verified
  • Barriers identified and addressed
Phase 4

Design and Build the Assessment

Effectiveness and Efficiency

  • Are the assessments effective in measuring intended outcomes?
  • Is the time investment proportional to the learning value?
  • Can the assessment be administered and marked efficiently?

Assessment Optionality and Adjustments

  • Are there alternative assessment options for students requiring adjustments?
  • Is there flexibility in assessment format while maintaining standards?
  • How are reasonable adjustments implemented?

Technology and GenAI Considerations

  • How do learning technologies affect student engagement with tasks?
  • What role does GenAI play in the assessment process?
  • Are there appropriate guidelines for technology use?
  • How is academic integrity maintained in digital environments?

Key Reflection Questions

  • What learning technologies are currently used and how do they affect assessment experiences?
  • Are there alternative ways to assess learning that might be more effective?
  • Do uses of GenAI affect student engagement with learning and assessment as planned?
  • How can technology enhance rather than hinder the assessment process?
Phase 4 Checklist
  • Effectiveness tested
  • Efficiency optimised
  • Adjustments available
  • Technology integration planned
  • GenAI guidelines established

Implementation Principles

Iterative Design

The framework operates as a cycle, not a linear process. Each phase informs and can trigger revision of previous phases. Continuous improvement through feedback loops.

Student Partnership

Students as co-designers in assessment planning. Regular consultation on assessment experiences. Transparency in assessment purposes and processes. Development of assessment literacy for all participants.

Evidence-Centred Approach

Decisions grounded in educational research. Data-informed modifications and improvements. Clear documentation of design rationale. Regular evaluation of assessment effectiveness.

Inclusive Practice

Universal Design for Learning principles. Cultural responsiveness in assessment design. Multiple means of demonstrating learning. Proactive removal of barriers to engagement.

Using the Framework

For New Assessment Design

  • Start with Phase 1 to establish clear foundations
  • Progress through each phase systematically
  • Document decisions and rationale at each stage
  • Review and revise based on validation outcomes
  • Implement with monitoring and adjustment mechanisms

For Existing Assessment Review

  • Begin with Phase 3 validation to identify current issues
  • Work backwards to identify root causes in earlier phases
  • Make targeted improvements based on findings
  • Re-validate after implementing changes
  • Document lessons learned for future iterations

For Continuous Improvement

  • Regular review cycles aligned with course evaluation
  • Student feedback integration after each assessment cycle
  • Peer review of assessment practices
  • Professional development in assessment design
  • Sharing of best practices across teaching teams
