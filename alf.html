How ADA uses the ALF

ADA treats Foundations as highest priority, then Norms, then Good Practice Guidance. ALF criteria are surfaced naturally as they become relevant — never all at once. They are raised as prompts for reflection, never as prescriptive rules. Where a criterion is relevant, ADA asks the questions listed at the end of each entry below.

Foundations

F1

Assessment Visualisation and Review

Programme teams work with students to review assessment visualisations and data to better understand typical student assessment experiences, with a view to reducing and smoothing any assessment overload.

Visualisations should include four elements:

  • Summative Assessment Summary: module code, assessment type, weighting, and submission deadline for each assessment.
  • Assessment Load Summary: total assessment hours per semester as a percentage of notional learning hours.
  • Programme Learning Outcome Review: which assessments address which PLOs.
  • Linked Formative Assessment Summary: the type of any linked formative opportunity preceding each summative assessment, its timing, and type of feedback.

The CHART PowerBI tool is the recommended university tool for generating these visualisations. Assessment hours data is entered in the Hours tab of each module record on Worktribe using the "Completion of assessment task" category. PLO mapping may be found in Programme Review SharePoint repositories or under the curriculum map tab on Worktribe.

Once created, visualisations should be reviewed by the programme team and with students to manage load, coordinate deadlines, reduce congestion, and design formative scaffolding.

ADA Prompt Has the team reviewed a programme-level visualisation of assessment recently? Do they have a clear picture of load, deadline distribution, and PLO coverage across the whole programme? Are students involved in reviewing this data?
F2

Assessment Load Cap

Students should be able to fit their summative assessment work comfortably within 30% of the total notional learning hours per semester.

For a standard 120-credit semester this equates to approximately 120–180 assessment hours. This Foundation is explicitly student-centred — reduced assessment load is the most desired change students have expressed — and success should be measured through direct discussion with students, not purely numerical calculation.

What counts as assessment hours

  • Research and preparation (understanding the brief and rubrics, detailed reading, data collection, note-taking)
  • Completing the assessment task itself (writing, creating, practising, performing)
  • Revision and editing/refinement of work
  • Preparing for and sitting examinations
  • Engaging with structured formative assessment opportunities

What does NOT count

  • Lectures, seminars, workshops, lab sessions
  • Background reading for module understanding not tied to a specific assessment
  • General study time not directly related to a specific assessment task

How to estimate assessment hours

Use the N4 Assessment Type Equivalences to estimate hours for each summative task; add hours for structured formative assessment; calculate total hours across all modules in the semester; consult students to validate estimates; adjust to stay within 120–180 hours. Enter estimates in Worktribe under the Hours tab.

Handling option modules

For programmes with option modules: focus on common student pathways; design core/compulsory modules with headroom (maximum approximately 40 assessment hours each) to leave room for variation in option module hours; provide transparency about option module loads so students can make informed choices.

For dissertations and major projects, only assessment-specific activity counts toward the cap (writing up, analysis for submission, preparing for oral examination).

ADA Prompt Has the team estimated assessment hours per module and checked the total against the 30% cap? Have students confirmed whether these estimates reflect their experience? For programmes with options, have common pathways been checked?
F3

Coursework Deadline Management

Smoothing of deadlines at semester level to reduce assessment clustering.

There is a strong student preference for a maximum of one or two summative coursework deadlines per student per week across the whole programme. This Foundation reflects significant student consultation and co-design.

When clustering cannot be avoided

It is more manageable for students if multiple deadlines in one week are: infrequent and not a regular pattern; due to legitimate constraints such as PSRB timing, professional practice windows, or external partner availability; scheduled during non-teaching weeks such as reading or consolidation weeks; or scheduled during end-of-semester assessment periods with due consideration for total load and exam timing.

Mitigation strategies

Where deadline spacing cannot be accommodated, mitigation strategies should be agreed with students in advance:

  • Reduced word counts or modified assessment formats for affected weeks
  • Early publication of clear assessment briefs (minimum four weeks before deadline)
  • Additional linked formative support in weeks preceding clustered deadlines
  • Assessment self-deferral options (students can defer one deadline by up to two weeks without penalty)
ADA Prompt How are deadlines currently distributed across the semester? Has the team reviewed the CHART deadline visualisation? Where clustering exists, have mitigation strategies been agreed with students?
F4

Formative Assessment

Summative assessment should be preceded by linked formative activities which support learning, develop assessment literacy or help students prepare for and succeed in the associated summative assessment.

A linked formative activity is not typically a full draft of the summative assessment. It is a purposeful activity that directly supports the final task — for example: a plan for the task; a specific section of the task; a discussion on scale, scope, direction or strategy; or a chance to develop a skill that will be used directly in the summative work.

The aim is to allow students to master one or more components, work with rubrics, receive feedback, and build confidence and independence (Psyllou, Dawood & Suleymenova, 2023). Feedback from formative activities must be timely enough for students to act on it before the summative deadline.

Formative assessment should add genuine value, not additional burden. It counts toward the F2 assessment load cap only when it is structured and requires meaningful student effort.

ADA Prompt Does each summative assessment have a clearly linked formative activity? Is the feedback loop timely? Does the formative activity genuinely scaffold the summative task rather than simply duplicating it?
F5

Examination Duration Standard

Closed-room examinations to be of either 1 hour or 2 hours duration.

Intermediate lengths such as 90 minutes or 40 minutes create resourcing and scheduling complexity and are less accessible for students with additional exam requirements (AERs) such as extra time or rest breaks. Longer or non-standard durations add disproportionately to exam stress and reduce time available for other activities. Standard durations allow AERs to work effectively and fairly.

Any departure from the one-hour or two-hour standard requires documented justification based on PSRB or regulatory requirements. Teams should work with their admin teams on how non-standard durations are handled. Where exam formats are being reviewed, teams should also consider alternative secure assessment approaches.

ADA Prompt Are any exams on this programme not one or two hours in duration? If so, is there documented regulatory justification? Have accessibility implications been considered and discussed with students?
F6

Student Partnership

Students to be involved as partners in assessment and feedback design for their programme.

Students should be involved as genuine partners in assessment and feedback design throughout the process — not merely consulted after decisions have already been made. The university distinguishes between two levels of involvement:

  • Co-design: working with students close to the start of a project so their ideas influence and can change the direction of initial planning — collaborative rather than consultative.
  • Partnership: students are involved throughout the entire project, from initial planning through to creation, delivery and evaluation, acting as partners at every stage.

Partnership is recommended for designing the overall assessment strategy and calibrating assessment load and scheduling. Co-design may be more suitable for developing the detail of specific assessments such as defining marking criteria and rubrics. The level of involvement should be agreed with students themselves.

ADA Prompt At what level are students currently involved in assessment design — consultation, co-design, or partnership? Is there an opportunity to deepen involvement? If students are not present in this conversation, how will their voice be incorporated?

Norms

N1

Programme-Level Assessment Design Approach

Adopt a programme-level assessment approach to manage and reconsider overall assessment load and focus.

Assessment should be designed coherently across the whole programme, not module by module in isolation. Programme-level design means: clear developmental progression across years; programme and year-level learning outcomes drive design, not just module outcomes; assessment tasks build on each other with actionable feedback feeding forward; and diversity of assessment type and feedback method is considered across the programme.

Practical steps

  • Create a whole-programme and year-part visual map using the CHART tool, starting with core/compulsory modules.
  • Invite students to review the map through their experience lens, focusing on workload patterns, clarity of progression, and opportunities for enhancement.
  • Embed low-stakes formative checkpoints directly connected to summative knowledge and skills required.
  • Run an Assessment in Motion (AIM) workshop to map assessments and analyse the knowledge and skills developmental journey.
  • Look for thematic or skill-based links across modules that could form the basis of synoptic or integrated tasks.
  • Use programme maps to remove unnecessary repetition of PLOs.
Case Study

Mapping at Programme Level — History (BA Hons, FAH)

Context

The History programme team, driven by concerns around GenAI and employability, wanted to diversify assessment to develop student skill sets and offer formats better suited to addressing GenAI challenges.

What they did

After a GenAI awareness workshop, the team ran a two-hour Assessment in Motion (AIM) workshop bringing staff and students together. AIM is a practical, card-based tool designed to identify current and proposed assessment types at module and programme level.

Impact

The AIM workshop led to redesigning five core Year 1 modules and two Year 2 modules, introducing oral assessment formats including academic conversations and presentations, embedding formative scaffolding through peer assessment and history lab hours, reducing assessment numbers in 15-credit modules, and piloting pass/fail elements and portfolio models.

What they learned

The task is not to combat AI but to equip staff and students to use it critically, reflectively, and with confidence. A phased approach built clarity and turned an overwhelming area into a manageable and constructive process.

ADA Prompt Is assessment currently designed at programme level or does each module operate independently? Is there a shared sense of how assessments build on each other across the student journey? Are programme-level learning outcomes explicitly driving assessment design choices?
N2

Assessment Number Range

Programmes should typically have between 4 and 16 summative assessments per 120-credit Part.

The minimum of 4 ensures assessment variety, regular feedback opportunities, and statistical validity for progression and classification decisions. The maximum of 16 prevents over-assessment — too many assessments fragments learning and creates excessive workload for students and staff. Every summative assessment carries significant workload consequences for academic and professional services staff.

When moving from many smaller assessments toward the 4–16 range, weightings will increase, but students will benefit from formative scaffolding and are likely to face fewer clustered deadlines.

For portfolio-based programmatic assessment, a single 120-credit portfolio should contain a minimum of 4 separately scored components to ensure statistical validity for progression decisions.

For programmes using workbooks, lab reports, or portfolios as established practice, teams should review opportunities to move some activity to formative assessment or learning checkpoints, reduce duplication, and consolidate small fractional elements.

ADA Prompt How many summative assessments does a student face per Part? If outside the 4–16 range, what is the rationale? Have options for consolidation, reduction, or moving some elements to formative been genuinely explored?
N3

Closed-Room Examination Practice

Reduce the number of closed-room examinations where possible.

Programmes should actively seek to reduce the number of closed-room examinations where possible. Teams should begin by identifying which closed-room exams are genuinely non-negotiable due to PSRB or regulatory requirements and document these clearly.

For all others, alternative secure assessment approaches should be considered — a variety of secure assessment methods using observed, unobserved, and hybrid approaches are emerging across the sector. Many PSRBs are simultaneously recognising the importance of AI fluency for graduate employability.

The CHART PowerBI visualisations (available after completing F1) show the current number and distribution of exams across a programme. The number and distribution of closed-room exams should also be reviewed against F3 deadline management principles.

ADA Prompt How many closed-room exams does a student face across the programme? Which are genuinely PSRB-mandated and documented as such? For those that are not mandated, have alternative secure assessment formats been genuinely considered?
N4

Assessment Type Equivalences

Assessment size (word count etc.) maintained to within specified limits.

Advisory word counts and equivalences are upper limits — shorter, higher-quality work is often preferable and can demonstrate more sophisticated skills. Word counts are designed to guide students toward expected depth, not to penalise students who produce shorter or longer work unless there is a specific learning-related purpose.

Good Practice Guidance

G1

Integrated/Synoptic Assessment

Design some assessments that span modules or scaffold skills development year on year.

Integrated or synoptic assessments are conceived across a whole programme and span two or more modules, creating meaningful connections between areas of learning and feeding constructively forward into future work. This approach reduces overall assessment volume while deepening learning.

Integrated assessment works best for programmes or Parts with mainly core or compulsory module clusters, or clearly defined thematic pathways. The modular structure can remain — integrated elements connect across modules and parts without replacing them. Students experience clearer connections between modules and benefit from reduced assessment volume and bunching.

ADA Prompt Are there opportunities to design assessments that draw on learning from more than one module, or that explicitly build on work from a previous year? Would this reduce overall assessment volume while deepening learning? Are there module clusters or thematic pathways that would lend themselves to this approach?
G2

Assessment Type Diversity

Diversify assessment formats; reduce over-reliance on one type; offer optionality where valid and feasible.

Programmes should actively diversify assessment formats, reducing over-reliance on any single type — particularly essays and closed-room exams — and offering student choice or optionality where valid and feasible. Diverse assessment strategies are more inclusive, test a wider range of skills, allow better alignment between assessment type and Programme Learning Outcomes, and are more engaging for students.

Teams should review current assessment type distribution using the CHART PowerBI tool and consider introducing formats such as oral assessments, presentations, portfolios, practical work, video production, posters, or group tasks where these better serve the learning outcomes. Disciplinary context and professional relevance should guide format choices — not convention or habit.

ADA Prompt Is there an over-reliance on one assessment format across this programme? Are there PLOs that could be better assessed through a different format? Has student choice or optionality been considered where it is pedagogically valid and practically feasible?
G3

Assessment Purpose Communication

Adopt the TILT methodology for clarifying and communicating assessment purpose.

Programmes should adopt the TILT (Transparency in Learning and Teaching) methodology to clarify and communicate the purpose of each assessment to students. See the dedicated TILT page for full detail.

ADA Prompt Does each assessment brief explicitly and separately state its purpose, a complete task description, and clear criteria for success? Have students been given the opportunity to respond to the brief and have misunderstandings been addressed before the assessment goes live?
G4

AI-Integrated Assessment Design

Consider embedding AI fluency and discipline-specific AI capabilities within Programme Learning Outcomes and design some assessments that require students to demonstrate effective, critical, and ethical use of AI tools.

Programmes should proactively consider how generative AI affects assessment integrity and student learning, and where appropriate, design assessments that explicitly embed AI use. This means:

  • Designing some assessments where appropriate AI use is permitted, expected, and scaffolded rather than prohibited
  • Developing student AI fluency as a named programme-level outcome
  • Producing clear discipline-specific guidance for students on appropriate, ethical AI use in their field

Student use of AI is already ubiquitous and graduates are entering an AI-enabled workplace — programmes that do not address this produce graduates who are neither AI-fluent nor equipped to use it ethically. Proactively embedding AI also reduces the risk of inappropriate use by giving students a legitimate, guided route.

Where AI use would undermine the learning purpose, teams should explain this clearly to students rather than simply prohibiting it without rationale.

ADA Prompt Has the team explicitly discussed how generative AI affects the integrity and purpose of each assessment? Are there assessments where AI use could be scaffolded and made part of the learning? Does the programme currently develop any AI fluency outcomes? Is there clear discipline-specific guidance for students on appropriate AI use?
G5

Programmatic Assessment

Consider the redesign of some programmes and their assessments using programmatic assessment methodology.

Programmatic assessment is a holistic approach in which assessment spans the entire academic year and feedback — rather than grades — is given at designated development points. Progression decisions are made against multiple sources of evidence rather than individual high-stakes assessments.

Key principles

  • Multiple low-stakes development points used to gather rich evidence about student learning
  • High-stakes summative decisions made only after holistic review of many development points
  • Students learn, re-learn, and re-work before a pass/fail summative portfolio submission at year end
  • A formal Mid-Year Review allows students to analyse their learning journey and co-create development plans with tutors
  • Programmatic assessment does not necessarily remove grades but discourages grades for every element

Students in Southampton pilots report valuing learning without the pressure of multiple high-stakes assessment points and have not reported missing grades, because they receive clear and regular feedback on whether they are on or off track. Tutors report students connecting concepts across topics rather than learning in siloed ways, and note increased engagement and attendance.

This is a significant redesign commitment requiring rethinking the entire programme's assessment architecture. Challenges include student records adaptations, board of examiners adjustments, staff mindset shifts, and regulatory flexibility. Any programme teams considering this approach must contact the CHEP Assessment Consultancy (AdvancingAssessment@soton.ac.uk) before making design decisions.

Case Study

Programmatic Assessment — WSA Fashion Marketing with Management, and Games Design & Art

Context

Winchester School of Art (WSA) piloted programmatic assessment with two programmes: BA Fashion Marketing with Management and BA Games Design & Art. Pilots were developed in close collaboration with the CHEP Assessment Consultancy and Student Interns.

What they did

The pilots adopted a model inspired by Netherlands-based programmatic assessment, characterised by multiple low-stakes feedback points, holistic judgement at programme level, and emphasis on feedback for learning. A formal Mid-Year Review was incorporated where students analysed their learning journey and co-created development plans with tutors.

Impact

Academics reported students connecting concepts across topics rather than approaching learning in siloed ways. Students highlighted the clearer structure and purpose and valued learning without the immediate pressure of high-stakes assessment during the difficult Semester 1 transition period. Tutors reported that teaching within this model was more enjoyable and meaningful.

What they learned

Several "unknown unknowns" emerged around aligning institutional systems — student records, boards of examiners, regulatory flexibility — but collaborative problem solving between academic and professional service colleagues ensured workable solutions.

ADA Prompt Is the team open to exploring a fundamentally different model of assessment where the programme year is one integrated learning journey? Are there specific drivers — high student grade anxiety, fragmented feedback, poor feed-forward — that make this worth exploring? Is the team aware this requires significant redesign commitment and institutional support?
