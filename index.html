ADA

Adaptive AI Agent for Assessment Design

ADA is a pedagogical consultant and critical friend that facilitates collaborative, inclusive assessment design between staff and students — grounded in the Southampton Assessment Load Framework and evidence-based practice.

View the Framework ALF Criteria

What ADA Does

ADA supports programme teams and students in reviewing, reflecting on, and improving assessment practices. It facilitates structured discussion, prompts critical reflection, and assists decision-making — never replacing human judgment, always stimulating it.

◈

Facilitates

Structured collaborative dialogue between staff and students on assessment design decisions.

The Southampton Assessment Load Framework (ALF) and four core theoretical foundations.

Supports

Fair, inclusive, and evidence-informed assessment through constructive critical questioning.

Assessment Load Framework Overview

The Southampton ALF is structured across three tiers. ADA prioritises Foundations first, then Norms, then Good Practice Guidance.

Foundations

Evidence-based practices with the greatest impact on student workload and wellbeing. Strongest institutional expectation.

  • F1 Assessment Visualisation and Review
  • F2 Assessment Load Cap
  • F3 Coursework Deadline Management
  • F4 Formative Assessment
  • F5 Examination Duration Standard
  • F6 Student Partnership

Norms

Effective practice based on educational research and sector standards, aimed at reducing unsustainable workload.

  • N1 Programme-Level Assessment Design
  • N2 Assessment Number Range
  • N3 Closed-Room Examination Practice
  • N4 Assessment Type Equivalences

Good Practice Guidance

Developmental and innovative approaches for teams ready to go further.

  • G1 Integrated/Synoptic Assessment
  • G2 Assessment Type Diversity
  • G3 Assessment Purpose Communication
  • G4 AI-Integrated Assessment Design
  • G5 Programmatic Assessment

Theoretical Foundations

ADA draws on these frameworks to ground options and guidance, referencing them when offering suggestions or critical perspectives.

Assessment Design

Bearman, M., Dawson, P., Boud, D., Bennett, S., Hall, M., & Molloy, E. (2016). Assessment Design Decisions Framework. Teaching in Higher Education, 21(5), 545–556.

Evidence-Centred Design

Huff, K., Steinberg, L., & Matts, T. (2010). Evidence-Centered Assessment Design. Applied Measurement in Education, 23(4), 310–324.

Assessment Literacy

Quilter, S. M., & Gallini, J. K. (2000). Teachers' and Students' Assessment Literacy. The Teacher Educator, 36(2), 115–131.

Student Partnership

Deeley, S. J., & Bovill, C. (2015). Student Partnership in Assessment and Feedback. Assessment & Evaluation in Higher Education, 42(3), 463–477.

Formative Assessment

Psyllou, M., Dawood, M., & Suleymenova, K. (2023). Scaffolded Formative Assessment: Fit for Purpose? Education in Practice, 4(2), 54–65.
